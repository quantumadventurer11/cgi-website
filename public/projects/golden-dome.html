Active research project

Golden Dome: international law, space security, and strategic stability.

An Institute research program examining the legal and strategic implications of proposed space-based missile-defence capabilities, dual-use infrastructure, and counterspace effects.

Project overview

A system-of-systems legal inquiry.

Golden Dome is being studied as a system-of-systems rather than a single weapon. Public materials describe an architecture involving sensors, command-and-control, and terrestrial and space-based interception capabilities.

The Institute is examining international space law, the law governing the use of force, the law of armed conflict, state responsibility, liability, orbital sustainability, and strategic stability. The intended output is a public legal and policy report.

Why this matters

Missile defence in orbit touches law, infrastructure, and crisis stability.

Strategic stability

Nuclear deterrence, compressed decision times, crisis instability, pre-emption incentives, and counterspace incentives shape how a defensive architecture may be perceived.

Orbital environment

Debris risk, harmful interference, dual-use ambiguity, and proximity operations can affect peaceful access to outer space.

Civil dependencies

Civilian and commercial space systems increasingly support communications, navigation, timing, disaster response, and economic activity.

Known and unresolved

Separate the public record from non-public design choices.

What the public record establishes

  • Golden Dome is described as an integrated missile-defence architecture.
  • Public planning materials identify space-based sensing and interception capabilities.
  • Kinetic and non-kinetic capabilities across multiple domains are contemplated.

What remains unresolved or non-public

  • Final basing modes, quantities, autonomy levels, and targeting rules.
  • Deployment schedules and the distinction between proposed and selected components.
  • Operational command arrangements, testing parameters, and selected target sets.

Preliminary legal propositions

Questions for analysis, not final Institute conclusions.

01

The Outer Space Treaty does not impose a blanket prohibition on every conventional weapon placed in Earth orbit.

02

Article IV expressly prohibits nuclear weapons and other weapons of mass destruction in orbit, while broader restrictions apply to the Moon and other celestial bodies.

03

A defensive label does not determine legal status.

04

Legal assessment depends on design, deployment context, target set, intended use, command arrangements, operational conduct, and effects.

05

A capability may be lawful to research or deploy but unlawful to test or use in a particular manner.

06

Dual-use sensors, servicing systems, proximity capabilities, and command infrastructure may create legal and strategic risks even if not independently classified as weapons.

07

Existing law regulates important parts of the problem but may not provide a comprehensive arms-control framework for conventional orbital interceptors.

Frameworks

Lifecycle, capability, and scenario analysis.

Research and development

Distinguishes concept development, legal characterization, procurement signals, and technology readiness from deployment or use.

Deployment

Examines basing mode, orbital sustainability, registration, liability, command arrangements, and strategic signaling.

Testing

Assesses debris, interference, notification, target characterization, and environmental risk.

Peacetime operation

Reviews due regard, supervision, dual-use ambiguity, proximity behavior, and civilian dependency risk.

Crisis and armed conflict

Connects capability effects to the UN Charter, law of armed conflict, self-defense, attribution, and escalation dynamics.

Detection and tracking

Space-based sensing, cueing, data fusion, and dual-use surveillance implications.

Physical interception

Kinetic interceptors, debris effects, target status, and proportionality questions.

Directed energy and electronic effects

Reversible interference, damage thresholds, attribution, and warning.

Co-orbital and proximity systems

Inspection, servicing, rendezvous, and counterspace ambiguity.

Decision and control architecture

Autonomy, command authority, escalation control, and accountability.

Stability and offensive potential

How defensive architecture may produce offensive or destabilizing incentives.
