Modernizing the Outer Space Treaty

Gap Analysis, Comparative State Practice, and Expert Perspectives on Reform Pathways

Download PDF Open PDF

Abstract

A careful reform agenda for the constitutional treaty of outer space.

Modernizing the Outer Space Treaty examines how the 1967 treaty framework is strained by space resources, commercial operations, dual-use systems, environmental risk, and diverging national implementation. The report treats the treaty as a durable constitutional foundation while identifying where clarification, codification, and institutional support are needed.

The analysis combines treaty interpretation, comparative state practice, and expert perspectives to identify practical reform pathways for multilateral discussion. Its orientation is incremental and preservationist: strengthen the existing legal order before fragmentation becomes the default operating condition.

Key findings

  1. The OST remains foundational, but several provisions face interpretative stress under contemporary activity.
  2. Resource extraction, continuing supervision, harmful interference, liability, and environmental protection require clearer operational baselines.
  3. National regimes are moving faster than multilateral clarification, creating risks of legal divergence.
  4. The most practical reform pathway is codification of emerging consensus, supported by soft-law guidance and targeted institutional mechanisms.
  5. Expert consultation supports qualitative insight, not statistical claims.

Recommendations

  1. Prioritize COPUOS-facing codification of agreed baseline obligations.
  2. Clarify environmental duties, debris mitigation, and harmful interference standards.
  3. Develop coordinated licensing principles for resource activities and commercial operations.
  4. Strengthen registration, transparency, and neutral depositary functions through a UN-linked mechanism.
  5. Expand future consultation to a broader and more geographically representative expert community.
Methodology note

Six survey submissions were received. Three identical duplicate submissions were removed, leaving four effective respondents. Because of the small sample, the consultation should be treated as qualitative expert input rather than a statistically representative survey.
