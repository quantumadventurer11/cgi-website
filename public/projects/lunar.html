Project overview

Institutional design for durable lunar activity.

The Lunar Project examines how governance systems could evolve from present-day international law and space activity into durable institutions for settlement, infrastructure, resource management, public administration, health, safety, dispute resolution, environmental stewardship, commercial coordination, and international representation.

The project is not a claim that settlements or public authorities already exist on the Moon. It uses scenario planning to test what institutions may be needed if activity grows from missions into sustained communities.

Why this matters

Governance questions arrive before permanent settlement.

Coordination pressure

Fragmented governance, infrastructure coordination, resource competition, safety, and public health may become practical problems as activity increases.

Legitimacy and continuity

Representation, accountability, dispute resolution, legal continuity, and international legitimacy matter before operational practices harden into expectations.

Long-term sustainability

Environmental stewardship, conflict prevention, and durable public administration are governance problems, not only technical mission problems.

Current context

Separate present activity from modeled futures.

What exists now

  • Current treaty framework and national authorization systems.
  • Space-agency programs, commercial missions, and current lunar exploration activity.
  • Technical constraints around transport, power, communications, life support, dust, and surface operations.

What the project models

  • Future settlements, governance bodies, legal institutions, and public services.
  • City systems, resource-management mechanisms, and inter-settlement coordination.
  • Hypothetical planning functions clearly separated from present-day facts.

Lunar governance should develop incrementally from current international law.

Institutional legitimacy requires transparency, representation, accountability, and legal continuity.

03

Settlement governance cannot be reduced to property or resource law.

Health, infrastructure, safety, dispute resolution, and environmental stewardship are governance questions.

Governance models must remain adaptable to actual future missions, treaties, and institutions.

06

Hypothetical planning should be clearly separated from present-day facts.

Hypothetical institution

Lunar Administration

The Lunar Administration is a proposed governance concept for CGI scenario work. It is not an existing public body, treaty organ, or operational authority.

REG

Registry and transparency office

Maintain public records, notices, operator filings, and settlement activity logs.

LZ

Landing zone coordination

Coordinate landing areas, traffic windows, safety notices, and exclusion timing.

SAFE

Safety and emergency coordination

Support rescue protocols, emergency authority, warnings, and shared risk reporting.

GRID

Infrastructure access

Set fair-use rules for power, communications, mobility corridors, and shared systems.

SCI

Scientific site protection

Protect high-value research zones, instrument fields, samples, and open-data practices.

RES

Resource activity interface

License resource-support activity, monitor impacts, and coordinate scarcity rules.

ENV

Environmental monitoring

Review contamination risks, dust impacts, waste handling, and site-preservation data.

MED

Dispute intake and mediation

Provide an early channel for operational conflicts before escalation becomes political.

STD

Standards coordination

Align habitat, mobility, communications, safety-buffer, and public-reporting practices.

Sustainable space operations

Governance that keeps the operating environment usable.

Sustainability is treated as an operations discipline: fewer avoidable conflicts, clearer duties, and shared infrastructure rules before fragile sites become crowded.

Dust

Dust and contamination control for landing, mobility, habitats, and instrument fields.

Waste

End-of-life planning for equipment, packaging, surface debris, and abandoned systems.

Traffic

Landing-zone deconfliction, safety buffers, route notices, and emergency priority.

Power

Shared power, communications, and corridor access rules for scarce infrastructure.

Science

Protection of high-value research sites, samples, sensors, and open-data practices.

Resources

Transparent resource-use reporting, monitoring, and scarcity review.

Rescue

Emergency and rescue obligations for public missions, private operators, and visitors.

Heritage

Preservation of historic, cultural, and scientifically significant surface locations.

Methodology and evidence standard

Scenario planning must stay traceable.

The project uses scenario planning, institutional design, legal analysis, systems thinking, comparative governance, technical constraints, phased assumptions, expert consultation, and a real-world update mechanism. The model should adapt when actual missions, settlements, treaties, or institutions emerge.
