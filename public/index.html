Mission & Vision

Mission

CGI produces rigorous, peer-reviewed space governance research aimed at submission to the UN Committee on the Peaceful Uses of Outer Space (COPUOS) and publication in academic journals. We work to close the widening gap between a space economy that has outpaced its 1967-era legal framework and the international institutions meant to govern it.

Vision

A multilateral, democratic, rule-of-law order for outer space and celestial bodies — one in which new frontiers remain under democratic rather than purely corporate or unilateral control, and in which the exploration and use of space is conducted as the common heritage of humankind.

Strategic Goals

Four lines of work for a modernized governance model.

01

Clarify Regulatory Ambiguities

categorize legal voids into normative gaps (no law exists) and interpretative gaps (law is ambiguous), producing a published Taxonomy of Gaps usable by policy advisors.

02

Harmonize Global Standards

contrast OST provisions against current domestic legislation of the major spacefaring powers to map specific points of friction.

03

Formulate Policy Pathways

develop distinct routes (treaty amendment, optional protocol, soft-law guidelines) toward a modernized governance model.

04

Bridge Industry and Policy

connect researchers, academia, and the commercial sector so recommendations are grounded in operational reality.

Current Projects

Research pathways from treaty analysis to lunar administration.

OSTGAP Status: Manuscript Delivered

Outer Space Treaty Gap Analysis Project

A seven-section, peer-reviewed paper analyzing gaps in the Outer Space Treaty across three phases: Phase 1, textual gap analysis of the treaty articles; Phase 2, comparative national legislation analysis of major spacefaring jurisdictions; Phase 3, a policy recommendations framework. Targets COPUOS submission and academic-journal publication. It examines the three articles under greatest interpretative stress — Article II (non-appropriation), Article VI (state responsibility and continuing supervision), and Article IX (harmful interference) — and advances a codification-first reform pathway grounded in the hardening of customary international law.

CGI-LUN-2026-001 Status: Phase 1, Active

The Lunar Project

A long-term governance and settlement roadmap for sustained multilateral human lunar presence across a 2030–2100 horizon. Structured around four named settlement nodes — Amity City, Mycenae, Hattusa, and Tycho — each with defined lunar coordinates and a build order (Hattusa first, co-locating peaks of eternal light with cryogenic water-ice cold traps). The proposed Lunar Administration is modeled on the International Seabed Authority as an independent treaty body.

Origins

Why we exist

The global space sector has outpaced the regulatory frameworks built during the Cold War. The 1967 Outer Space Treaty (OST) remains the constitution of space law, but the rise of non-state actors, mega-constellations exceeding 50,000 satellites, and imminent commercial lunar resource extraction have opened serious normative and interpretative gaps. Space governance today suffers from "vertical fragmentation": static international obligations on top, rapidly and divergently evolving national licensing regimes below. Without rigorous analysis identifying exactly where these frictions exist, the risk is a fragmented legal order governed by "might makes right" rather than the rule of law. CGI was founded to supply that evidentiary basis. What began as the Outer Space Treaty Gap Analysis within the Democracy and Federalism Hub grew into a standing initiative with a multidisciplinary, international team of lawyers, scientists, economists, and policy researchers.
